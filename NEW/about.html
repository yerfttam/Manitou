About Manitou Lodge

Our Story

A Lodge Built for the Forest

Originally built in 1970, Manitou Lodge was designed around a classic lodge structure whose most striking feature is a great room with vaulted ceilings and a massive stone fireplace. Set on ten acres of coastal rainforest — just about as far west as you can get in the lower 48 states — Manitou is what a lodge should be: wood, stone, and furniture you can put your feet up on.

Nights here are quiet except for the distant rush of the Sol Duc River and the occasional calls of coyotes and owls. We offer lodge rooms, cottage rooms, rustic cabins, A-frames, and camping for guests who want to be as close to the wild Washington coast as possible.

The Property

What Makes Manitou Special

  • Unique & Scenic Setting Ten acres of coastal rainforest, just about as far west as you can get in the lower 48 states.
  • Ancient Trees A Western Hemlock on the property is well over 1,000 years old, with imposing hemlocks and spruce throughout.
  • Trails & Grounds Wonderful walking trails, bits of whimsy, and lovely spots to picnic throughout the property.
  • Summer Weather Sunshine and temperatures that rarely rise above 80°F — ideal for exploring the coast and rainforest.
  • Winter Storms Powerful Pacific storms worth experiencing — especially when a couch, fire, and warm bed are just minutes away.
  • World-Class Fishing The Sol Duc, Bogachiel, Calawah, and Hoh Rivers offer exceptional salmon and steelhead fishing.
Explore

Forks & Olympic National Park

There is something for everybody any time of year in Olympic National Park and Forks, Washington. Explore wilderness beaches, hike through moss-draped rainforest, fish the plentiful rivers, or simply enjoy the quiet of the far west end of the Olympic Peninsula.

Manitou Lodge is ideally positioned as a base for exploring the entire coast — close to everything, yet surrounded by forest.

Beaches

The Coast

Rialto Beach — 3 miles away. A spectacular driftwood-strewn beach with heavy surf, huge logs, and magnificent sunsets. The crescent extends roughly 1.5 miles, ending with sea stacks, tidal pools, and "Hole-in-the-Wall." An 18-mile backpacking route heads north to Cape Alava.

First, Second, and Third Beaches — South of the Quileute River mouth near La Push. Long flat crescents popular with photographers for their sea stacks, the "Quileute Needles," tidal pools, and eagles. Second Beach is a 0.5-mile hike; Third Beach is 1.5 miles. You can backpack south roughly 17 miles to Oil City.

Rainforest

The Hoh Rainforest

About 40 miles from Manitou Lodge, the Hoh Rainforest receives over 150 inches of rain annually. The Hall of Mosses Trail winds through moss-draped big-leaf maples described by visitors as a jade-colored cathedral. The Spruce Trail follows the glacier-carved Hoh River through ancient forest. Scenic raft trips run in summer; fishing guides operate year-round.
